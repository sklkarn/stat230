    Section 2.1 Measure of Central Tendency

    In statistics, understanding the data is very import to draw any information and conclusion. Central tendency and dispersion help us know the data distribution and their characteristics. Central tendency is the descriptive summary of a data set. It aims to provide an accurate description of the entire data in the distribution. The central tendency of the dataset can be found using the three important measures namely mean, median and mode.
    An average of the data can be the best measurement of central tendency. There are two ways to determine an average of the data:
    1. Mathematical:
      1. Arithmatic Mean (Mean)
      2. Geometric Mean
      3. Harmonic Mean
    2. Positional:
      1. Median
      2. Mode

    Subsection 2.1.1 Mean (average)

    For a sample mean:
    \begin{equation} \bar{x} =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{x_i}{n}\tag{2.1.1} \end{equation}
    Here, \(x_i\) is the \(i^{th}\) observation of sample, and \(n\) is number of observations.
    For a Population mean:
    \begin{equation} \mu =\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{x_i}{N}\tag{2.1.2} \end{equation}
    Here, \(x_i\) is the \(i^{th}\) observation of population, and \(N\) is number of observations (population).
    In mean, numerical variables are affected by outliers or extreme values. For example:
    \begin{equation*} x = 2,4,6,8,10 \end{equation*}
    The mean of x
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x} = \frac{2+4+6+8+10}{5} = 6 \end{equation*}
    Now suppose,
    \begin{equation*} x=2,4,6,8,10,100 \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x} = \frac{2+4+6+8+10+100}{6} = 21.67. \end{equation*}
    Mean gives no information if there are any outliers in the data.
    There are three types of mean being used in mathematical operations: Arithmetic mean, geometric mean, and harmonic mean. Here we only discuss the arithmetic mean. There are three methods to determine the arithmetic mean.
    1. Direct Method
    2. Assumed Mean Method, and
    3. Step Deviation Method
    Here we are trying to build an idea to find mean of the three different data (series) using direct mean method:
    1. Individual Series
    2. Discrete Series, and
    3. Continuous Series.

    Subsubsection 2.1.1.1 Raw Data (individual series):

    The value of mean can be calculated as
    \begin{equation} \bar{x} =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{x_i}{n}\tag{2.1.3} \end{equation}
    Here, \(x_i\) is the \(i^{th}\) observation of sample, and \(n\) is number of observations.
    The mean (also known as the average) is the sum of all values divided by the total number of values. It provides a balanced representation of the entire dataset. To acknowledge that we are calculating the population mean and not the sample mean, we use the Greek lower case letter "mu", denoted as \(\mu\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \mu =\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{X_i}{N} \end{equation*}
    An important property of the mean is that it includes every value in your data set as part of the calculation. In addition, the mean is the only measure of central tendency where the sum of the deviations of each value from the mean is always zero.
    The mean of
    \begin{equation*} 4,8,7,15,18,20 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x} =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{x_i}{n} = \frac{4+8+7+15+18+20}{6} = 12. \end{equation*}
    The mean of ungrouped data
    \begin{equation*} 4,8,7, 0, 15,18,20 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x} =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{x_i}{n} = \frac{4+8+7+0+15+18+20}{7} = 10.22. \end{equation*}
    The mean of ungrouped data
    \begin{equation*} 12,12,12, 12, 12,12,12 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x} =\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{x_i}{n} = 12. \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 2.1.1.2 Discrete Series:

    The mean can be calculated using the following methods:
    1. Direct method:
      \begin{equation*} \bar{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{f_i x_i}{f_i} \end{equation*}
    2. Assumed mean method:
      \begin{equation} \bar{x} =A+ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{f_i d_i}{f_i}\tag{2.1.4} \end{equation}
      here, \(A\) is assumed mean (middle mean value), \(f_i\) is frequency of \(i^{th}\) observation, and \(d =x_i -A\) the deviation of observation, \(x_i.\)
    1. Direct method to calculate Mean of grouped data:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \circ & x_i & f_i & \\ \hline 1 & 7 & 2 \\ 2 & 11 & 3 \\ 3 & 15 & 6 \\ 4 & 10 & 4 \\ 5 & 12 & 2 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
      Here is the caculation:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c} \circ & x_i & f_i & f_ix_i & \\ \hline 1 & 7 & 2 & 14 \\ 2 & 11 & 3 & 33 \\ 3 & 15 & 6 & 90 \\ 4 & 10 & 4 & 40 \\ 5 & 12 & 2 & 24 \\ \hline Sum & & 17 & 201 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
      Now,
      \begin{equation*} \bar{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{f_i x_i}{f_i} = \frac{213}{17} =11.82. \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 2.1.1.3 Continuous Series:

    In continuous series, the observations are presented in different class intervals rather than by a single number and the frequency. It can be presented in two different types of class intervals: Exclusive series and Inclusive series. Continuous series includes the upper limit and the lower limit in the class interval. If the upper limit interval does not repeat itself as a lower limit of the next class interval then it is an inclusive series. There is a gap between upper limit of any class interval to the and lower limit of the next class interval. e.g., \(20-29, 30-39, 40-49, 50-59, etc.\) If the upper limit interval repeats itself as a lower limit of the next class interval then it is an exclusive series. There is no gap between the upper limit of any class interval to the and lower limit of the next class interval. e.g., \(20-30, 30-40, 40-50, 50-60, etc.\)
    The mean (weighted mean of grouped data) can be calculated using the following methods:
    1. Direct method:
      \begin{equation*} \bar{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{f_i x_i}{f_i}. \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c} \circ & class & f_i & x_i & f_ix_i \\ \hline 1 & 0-10 & 3 & 5 & 15 \\ 2 & 10-20 & 5 & 15 & 75 \\ 3 & 20-30 & 4 & 25 & 100 \\ 4 & 30-40 & 8 & 35 & 280 \\ 5 & 40-50 & 2 & 45 & 90 \\ \hline Sum & & 22 & & 560 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    2. Step deviation method: This method is only applicable for continuous series.
      \begin{equation} \bar{x} = A+\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{f_i d_i}{f_i}\right)h. \tag{2.1.5} \end{equation}
      here,
      \begin{equation} d= \frac{x_i-A}{h}\text{,}\tag{2.1.6} \end{equation}
      \(h=\) class interval.

    Subsubsection 2.1.1.4 Weighted (Combined) Mean

    \begin{equation*} \bar{x} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{n_i\bar{x_i}}{n_i} \end{equation*}
    Example 2.1.1.
    The mean weight of 15 students is 110 kg. The mean of 5 of them is 100 kg and another 5 kg of them is 125 kg, then the mean weight of remaining students is
    1. 120
    2. 105
    3. 115
    4. none.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} N\bar{x} \amp = n_1\bar{x_1} + n_2\bar{x_2} + n_3\bar{x_3} \\ 15\times 110 \amp = 5\times 100 + 5\times 125 + 5\times \bar{x_3} \\ \therefore \bar{x_3} \amp = (1650 -1125)/5 =105. \end{align*}
    here \(N=\sum_{i=1}^{n} n_i\)

    Subsection 2.1.2 Median

    Median, in statistics, is the middle value of the given list of data when arranged in an order. It is also called the positional average. In median, numerical variables are less influenced by outliers. For even number of items in data, median is the average of two middle values of the ordered data. For odd number of items in data, median is just the middle item in the ordered data. For example:
    \begin{equation*} x= 7,3,11,15,4,20,23 \end{equation*}
    after arranging it in increasing order
    \begin{equation*} x =3,4,7,11,15,20,23 \end{equation*}
    Here, \(11\) appeared at the middle term, so it is a median of the given data.
    Also,
    \begin{equation*} x= 12,18,4,9, 17,8 \end{equation*}
    after arranging it in increasing order
    \begin{equation*} x = 4,8,9,12,17,18 \end{equation*}
    Here, \(9\) and \(12\)appeared as the middle terms, so it’s median is average of these numbers.
    \begin{equation*} median = \frac{9+12}{2} = 10.5 \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 2.1.2.1 Raw Data (individual series)

    The median of the following data can be found by rearranging them either in ascending or in descending order then select middle item.
    1. \begin{equation*} 7,3,11,15,4,20,23 \end{equation*}
      after arranging it in increasing order
      \begin{equation*} 3,4,7,11,15,20,23 \end{equation*}
      Here, \(11\) appeared at the middle term, so it is a median of the given data.
    2. \begin{equation*} 12,18,4,9, 17,8 \end{equation*}
      after arranging it in increasing order
      \begin{equation*} 4,8,9,12,17,18 \end{equation*}
      Here, \(9\) and \(12\)appeared as the middle terms, so it’s median is average of these numbers.
      \begin{equation*} median = \frac{9+12}{2} = 10.5 \end{equation*}
    1. Median for odd number of terms is given by
      \begin{equation} median = \left(\frac{n+1}{2}\right)^{th} \text{ term}.\tag{2.1.7} \end{equation}
      here \(n\) is number of terms in a series.
    2. Median for even number of terms is given by
      \begin{equation} median = \frac{\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{th} \text{ term} + \left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)^{th} \text{ term} }{2}.\tag{2.1.8} \end{equation}
      here \(n\) is number of terms in a series.
    Example 2.1.2.
    If median of \(x, \frac{x}{2}, \frac{x}{3}, \frac{x}{5}\) is 10, then find the value of \(x.\)
    Solution.
    Arrange the series in order,
    \begin{equation*} x, \frac{x}{2}, \frac{x}{3}, \frac{x}{5} \end{equation*}
    Now, take average of two middle terms from even number series.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac{x}{2}+\frac{x}{3} }{2}=10 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \frac{\frac{3x+2x}{6} }{2}=10 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \frac{5x}{6}=20 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore x = 24. \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 2.1.2.2 Discrete Series (data)

    Find the median of the following series
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} x & f \\ \hline 5 & 4 \\ 9 & 2 \\ 3 & 1 \\ 15 & 3 \\ 11 & 5 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    First arrange the observations in order
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} x & f & CF \\ \hline 3 & 1 & 1 \\ 5 & 4 & 5 \\ 9 & 2 & 7 \\ 11 & 5 & 12 \\ 15 & 3 & 15 \\ \hline sun & 15 & \end{array} \end{equation*}
    \(CF\) is cumulative frequency. Number of observation, \(n = 15\) = odd. Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} median = \left(\frac{n+1}{2}\right)^{th} = 8^{th} \text{ term} \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(median = x = 11\text{.}\)
    Example 2.1.3.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} x & f \\ \hline 9 & 4 \\ 15 & 3 \\ 28 & 9 \\ 30 & 3 \\ 20 & 2 \\ 17 & 7 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} x & f & CF \\ \hline 9 & 4 & 4 \\ 15 & 3 & 7 \\ 17 & 7 & 14 \\ 20 & 2 & 16 \\ 28 & 9 & 25 \\ 30 & 3 & 28 \\ \hline sum & 28 & \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Median for even numbers observations
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{1}{2}\left[ \left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{th} + \left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)^{th} \right] = \frac{1}{2}\left[ 14^{th}+15^{th} \right] \text{ term} \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} median = \frac{1}{2}\left[17+20 \right] = 37/2 = 18.5. \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 2.1.2.3 Continuous Series

    \begin{equation} Median = L + \frac{1}{f}\left(\frac{N}{2}-f_o \right)\times h\tag{2.1.9} \end{equation}
    \(L\) = Lower limit of median class, \(N =\sum f\text{,}\) \(f_o \) upper CF, \(f\) = frequency, \(h=\) class interval.
    Example 2.1.4.
    Exclusive class series:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} class & f & CF \\ \hline 0-10 & 3 & 3 \\ 10-20 & 5 & 8 \\ 20-30 & 4 & 12 \\ 30-40 & 8 & 20 \\ 40-50 & 2 & 22 \\ \hline sum & 22 & \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Arrange the series in order \(h=10\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{N}{2} =22/2 =11^{th}. \end{equation*}
    Hence, the median class.
    \begin{equation*} \mbox{ [20-30 | 4 | 12] } \end{equation*}
    \(L =20, \) \(f_o = 8,\) \(h=10.\) from (2.1.9)
    \begin{equation*} median = 20+\frac{1}{4}\left(11-8 \right)\times 10 = 27.5. \end{equation*}
    Example 2.1.5.
    Inclusive class series:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} class & f \\ \hline 50-59 & 2 \\ 60-69 & 5 \\ 70-79 & 4 \\ 80-89 & 8 \\ 90-100 & 3 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Convert the inclusive class series into exclusive series
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} class & f & CF \\ \hline 49.5-59.5 & 2 & 2 \\ 59.5-69.5 & 5 & 7 \\ 69.5-79.5 & 4 & 11 \\ 79.5-89.5 & 8 & 19 \\ 89.5-99.5 & 3 & 22 \\ \hline sum & 22 & \end{array} \end{equation*}
    median class selection = \(\left(\frac{N}{2}\right)^{th} =22/2 =11^{th} \) term. Hence, the median class.
    \begin{equation*} \mbox{[ 69.5-79.5 | 4 | 11 ]} \end{equation*}
    from (2.1.9)
    \begin{equation*} median = 69.5 +\frac{1}{4}\left(11-7 \right)\times 10 = 79.5. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.1.3 Mode

    It is the most frequent value of the data. It is used for categoriacal variables.
    \begin{equation*} x= 5,6,10,12,4,6,10,15,10 \end{equation*}
    mode = 10 (unimodal)
    \begin{equation*} x= 2,7,9,15,9,3,7,5 \end{equation*}
    it has two most freuent data points, hence, the mode is \(7\) and \(9\) (Bimodal).
    \begin{equation*} x= 1,2,3,4,5 \end{equation*}
    Mode is not possible.

    Subsubsection 2.1.3.1 Individual Series

    1. \begin{equation*} 5,6,10,12,4,6,10,15,10 \end{equation*}
      mode = 10 (unimodal)
    2. \begin{equation*} 2,7,9,15,9,3,7,5 \end{equation*}
      it has two most freuent data points, hence, the mode is \(7\) and \(9\) (Bimodal)
    3. \begin{equation*} 1,2,3,4,5 \end{equation*}
      No mode possible.
    4. \begin{equation*} 4,5,6,4,5,6 \end{equation*}
      No modal possible.

    Subsubsection 2.1.3.2 Discrete Series

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} x & f \\ \hline 2 & 3 \\ 7 & 8 \\ 10 & 3 \\ 19 & 9 \\ 25 & 2 \\ 35 & 5 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Most frequent observation is \(19\) appeared \(9\) times. Hence,\(mode = 19\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} x & f \\ \hline 5 & 4 \\ 10 & 8 \\ 15 & 5 \\ 20 & 8 \\ 25 & 4 \\ 30 & 2 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Most frequent observation is \(10\) and \(20\) both appeared \(8\) times. Hence,\(mode = 10\) and \(20.\)

    Subsubsection 2.1.3.3 Continuous Series

    Exclusive class series:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} class & f \\ \hline 20-25 & 7 \\ 25-30 & 3 \\ 30-35 & 11 \\ 35-40 & 5 \\ 40-45 & 2 \\ 45-50 & 9 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} mode = L + \left(\frac{f_1-f_o}{2f_1-f_o-f_2}\right)h \end{equation*}
    L = lower limit of modal class, \(f_1 =\) frequency of modal class, \(f_o=\) frequency of pre-modal class, \(f_2 = \) frequency of post-modal class, \(h=\) class interval.
    Modal class is the highest frequency class data. In the given series, \(\mbox{[30-35 |11]} \) is the modal class.
    \begin{equation*} mode = 30 + \left(\frac{11-3}{2\times 11 -3-5}\right)\times 5 = 32.857. \end{equation*}
    Inclusive class series
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} class & f \\ \hline 20-39 & 6 \\ 40-59 & 4 \\ 60-79 & 3 \\ 80-99 & 7 \\ 100-119 & 10 \\ 120-139 & 5 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    convert the inclusive class into exclusive class.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} class & f \\ \hline 19.5-39.5 & 6 \\ 39.5-59.5 & 4 \\ 59.5-79.5 & 3 \\ 79.5-99.5 & 7 \\ 99.5-119.5 & 10 \\ 119.5-139.5 & 5 \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    now modal class = \(\mbox{[99.5-119.5 | 10]}\)
    therefore,
    \begin{equation*} mode= L + \left(\frac{f_1-f_o}{2f_1-f_o-f_2}\right)h = 99.5 + \left(\frac{10-7}{2\times 10 -7-5}\right)20 =107. \end{equation*}
    Properties of mode
    1. It is the most frequent value.
    2. Series can have unimodal, bimodal, multimodal, or no modal.
    3. It is the most unstable average.
    4. It is not based on all observations.
    5. It is not affected by extreme values.

    Subsection 2.1.4 Summary

    Mean is useful in numerical variables without outliers. In symmetric distribution of data mean is a good measure of central tendency.
    Median is useful in numerical variables with outliers. In skewed distribution of data median is a good measure of central tendency.
    Mode is useful in categorical variables.

    Subsection 2.1.5 Relation between Mean, Median, and Mode:

    Perfectly symmetric distribution
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} class & f \\ \hline 0-10 & 5 \\ 10-20 & 10 \\ 20-30 & 15 \\ 30-40 & 20 \\ 40-50 & 15 \\ 50-60 & 10 \\ 60-70 & 5 \\\hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    here calculated, \(mean = median = mode = 25\text{.}\)
    In perfectly symmetric distribution, mean, median, and mode are coinsident on one another. That is,
    \begin{equation*} Mean = Median = Mode. \end{equation*}
    Positively skewed distribution
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} class & f \\ \hline 0-10 & 5 \\ 10-20 & 15 \\ 20-30 & 40 \\ 30-40 & 25 \\ 40-50 & 20 \\ 50-60 & 10 \\ 60-70 & 5 \\\hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    here calculated, \(mean = 32.5\text{,}\) \(median= 30, \) and \(mode = 26.25\) Hence, in perfectly symmetric distribution,
    \begin{equation*} Mean = Median = Mode. \end{equation*}
    In positively skewed distribution,
    \begin{equation*} Mean \gt Median \gt Mode. \end{equation*}
    In skewed distribution, median always comes between mean and mode and follow the following relation.
    \begin{equation*} mode= 3(median)-2(mean). \end{equation*}
    Negatively skewed distribution
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} class & f \\ \hline 0-10 & 5 \\ 10-20 & 10 \\ 20-30 & 20 \\ 30-40 & 25 \\ 40-50 & 40 \\ 50-60 & 15 \\ 60-70 & 5 \\\hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    here calculated, \(mean = 37.5\text{,}\) \(median= 40, \) and \(mode = 43.75\text{.}\)
    In negatively skewed distribution,
    \begin{equation*} Mode \gt Median \gt Mean. \end{equation*}
    In skewed distribution, median always comes between mean and mode and follow the following relation.
    \begin{equation*} mode= 3(median)-2(mean). \end{equation*}

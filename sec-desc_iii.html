    Section 2.3 Dispersion (Spread, Variability)

    It is a measure of the extent to which the observations vary from some central value (mean, median, mode). It shows how the data are spread out (dispersed) from the central value. In simple terms, it shows how squeezed or scattered the variable is. It is a measure of degree of variation in the data. The following images show two data with same mean but different spread.
    For example: the mean of \(2,4,9\) is
    \begin{equation*} mean=\frac{\sum x}{n} = \frac{2+4+9}{3} =5. \end{equation*}
    Here, the dispersion shows how far are the observations from the mean (central value). The difference in each data point from the central value is represents deviation of the data.
    Suppose I have the three data set as shown below:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c} \circ & Series A (x) & Series B (x) & Series C (x) \\ \hline 1 & 100 & 98 & 1 \\ 2 & 100 & 99 & 2 \\ 3 & 100 & 100 & 3 \\ 4 & 100 & 101 & 4 \\ 5 & 100 & 102 & 490 \\ \hline Total & 500 & 500 & 500 \\ \hline \bar{x} & 100 & 100 & 100 \\ \hline Dispersion & No & Less & High \\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The data shows the difference in \(x\) and \(\bar{x}\text{.}\) the observations in series A does not vary at all from the central value (mean), there is some variation in series B, b ut no observations are even close to the mean in series C.

    Subsection 2.3.1 Measurement of Dispersion

    Dispersion can be measured using two different approaches.
    1. Absolute Measure (same unit).
      Absolute measures of dispersion use the original units of data, and are most useful on understanding the dispersion within the context of your experiment and measurements.
      1. Range
      2. Quartile Deviation
      3. Mean Deviation
      4. Standard Deviation
      5. Variance
    2. Relative Measure (unit free).
      In order to compare separate data sets or different experiments that might use different units and the difference in their central values are very high, we use relative measures.
      1. Coefficient of Range
      2. Coefficient of Quartile Deviation
      3. Coefficient of Mean Deviation
      4. Coefficient of Standard Deviation
      5. Coefficient of Variance
    Note: Low dispersion means more stable observations.

    Subsection 2.3.2 Range

    Range is the difference between the highest and the lowest value of observations for a given frequency distribution. The coefficient of range on the other hand is the ratio of difference between the highest and lowest value of observations to the sum of highest and lowest value of observations.
    \begin{equation} Range = L - S \tag{2.3.1} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} \text{Coefficient of Range} = \left(\frac{L-S}{L+S}\right) \times 100\%\tag{2.3.2} \end{equation}
    Here, \(L = \) largest observation, and \(S = \) smallest observation.
    Range is sensitive to extreme value but cannot give any information if there are extreme values in the data. It does not consider the distribution but suitable for small dataset. For the data \(\{1,2,3,4\}.\) Range = \(4-1=3.\)
    For the data \(\{1,2,3,4,50\}.\) Range = \(50-1=49.\)

    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} Range = 35-7 =28. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Coefficient of Range} = \left(\frac{35-7}{35+7}\right) \times 100 = 66.67\% \end{equation*}
    Example 2.3.3.

    \begin{equation*} \text{Coefficient of Range} = \left(\frac{92-20}{92+20}\right) \times 100 = 64.28\% \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.3.3 Quartiles

    Quartiles divide the data into four equal parts. Quartile deviation is a statistic that measures the deviation in the middle of the data. Quartile deviation is also referred to as the semi interquartile range and is half of the difference between the third quartile and the first quartile value. Quartiles divide a group of observations into four equal parts. The first quartile, denoted by \(Q_1\) is defined as the \(25^{th}\) percentile, the second quartile (\(Q_2\)) is defined as the \(50^{th}\) percentile (median), and the third quartile (\(Q_3\)) is defined as the \(75^{th}\) percentile.
    The formula for quartile deviation of the data is
    \begin{equation} Q.D = \frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{2}.\tag{2.3.3} \end{equation}
    The coefficient of quartile deviation is a measure of dispersion, which is used to describe the spread or distribution of data.
    Coefficient of quartile deviation,
    \begin{equation} CQD = \left(\frac{Q_3 - Q_1}{Q_3 + Q_1}\right) \times 100\%.\tag{2.3.4} \end{equation}
    Here, \(Q_1\) is first quartile (or, lower quartile),
    \begin{equation*} Q_1 = [(n+1)/4]^{th} \text{ item } \end{equation*}
    \(Q_2\) is second quartile (or, median),
    \begin{equation*} Q_2 = [(n+1)/2]^{th} \text{ item } \end{equation*}
    and \(Q_3\) is third quartile (or, upper quartile)
    \begin{equation*} Q_3 = [3(n+1)/4]^{th} \text{ item } \end{equation*}
    Where \(n\) represents the total number of observations in the given data set.
    For the data \(\{4,6,7,8,10,23,24\}\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} Q_1 = \frac{7+1}{4} = 2^{nd} \text{ item} = 6. \end{equation*}
    Percentiles: It divide the data into 100 parts.
    \begin{equation*} percentile = \frac{p}{100}\times (n+1)^{th} \text{ term}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 25^{th} percentile = \frac{25}{100}\times (n+1)^{th} \text{ term} = \frac{n+1}{4} \text{ item}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 50^{th} percentile = \frac{50}{100}\times (n+1)^{th} \text{ term} = \frac{n+1}{2} \text{ item}. \end{equation*}
    Inter Quartile Range (IQR):
    \begin{equation*} IQR = Q_3-Q_1 = 50\% \text{ of the data}. \end{equation*}
    It is less sensitive to extreme values.

    Subsubsection 2.3.3.1 Individual series

    \begin{equation*} 2,7,9,14,1 \end{equation*}
    arrange in order
    \begin{equation*} 1, 2,7,9,14 \end{equation*}
    Here \(Q_2= 7\text{,}\) which is median, \(Q_1= 2\) is first quartile, and \(Q_3 = 9\) is third quartile.
    Example 2.3.7.
    Find the quartiles and quartile deviation of the following data:
    \begin{equation*} 17, 2, 7, 27, 15, 5, 14, 8, 10, 24, 48, 10, 8, 7, 18, 28 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    First arrange the data into order
    \begin{equation*} 2, 5, 7, 7, 8, 8, 10, 10, 14, 15, 17, 18, 24, 27, 28, 48 \end{equation*}
    Number of data values = \(n = 16\text{.}\)
    Median of the even number data set using (2.1.8),
    \begin{align*} Q_2 \amp = \frac{1}{2}\{\left( \frac{n}{2}\right)^{th} term + \left( \frac{n}{2}+1\right)^{th} term \} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}\{ 8^{th} term + 9^{th} term \} \\ \therefore Q_2 \amp = \frac{10+14}{2} = 12. \end{align*}
    Now, lower half of the data is:
    \begin{equation*} 2, 5, 7, 7, 8, 8, 10, 10 \end{equation*}
    (even number of observations)
    \begin{equation*} Q_1 = \text{ Median of lower half of the data } \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = (1/2)[4^{th} \text{ term } + 5^{th} \text{ term}] = \frac{7+8}{2} =7.5. \end{equation*}
    Also, the upper half of the data is:
    \begin{equation*} 14, 15, 17, 18, 24, 27, 28, 48 \end{equation*}
    (even number of observations)
    \begin{equation*} Q_3 = \text{ Median of upper half of the data } \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = (1/2)[4^{th} \text{ term } + 5^{th} \text{ term}]= \frac{18+24}{2} =21. \end{equation*}
    Therefore the Quartile deviation,
    \begin{equation*} QD = \frac{1}{2}(Q_3-Q_1) = \frac{21-7.5}{2}=6.75. \end{equation*}
    using equation (2.3.3).
    and the coefficient of Quartile deviation using (2.3.4),
    \begin{equation*} CQD = \frac{Q_3-Q_1}{Q_3+Q_1}(100) =\frac{21-7.5}{21+7.5}(100) = 47.36\%. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.3.4 Standard Deviation, \(\sigma\) and Variance, \(\sigma^2\)

    Standard deviation tells how far a group of numbers (observation) is from the mean.
    Variance is the average of the squared differences of a group of numbers (observation) from the mean.
    Standard deviation is the square root of the variance. It is also called root mean square deviation. Standard deviation, Variance and their coefficients can be calculated using the following formulae:
    1. Actual Mean Method.
      For a population standard deviation:
      \begin{equation} \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{N}}\tag{2.3.6} \end{equation}
      \(N\) is total number of observations (population) and \(\mu\) is a population mean (2.1.2).
      For a sample standard deviation:
      \begin{equation} \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\tag{2.3.7} \end{equation}
      \(n\) is total number of observations (sample).
    2. Coefficient of standard deviation.
      \begin{equation} CSD = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}}\tag{2.3.8} \end{equation}
    3. Variance.
      \begin{equation} \sigma^2 = \frac{(x-\bar{x})^2}{N}\tag{2.3.9} \end{equation}
    4. Coefficient of Variance.
      \begin{equation} CV = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}}\times 100\%\tag{2.3.10} \end{equation}

    Subsubsection 2.3.4.1 Individual Series

    The standard deviation of the data below: \(10,12,13,15,20\)
    Actual Mean Method (direct method).
    from equation (2.1.3)
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n} = \frac{10+12+13+15+20}{5} = 70/5=14. \end{equation*}
    Now,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} x & x-\bar{x} &( x-\bar{x})^2 \\ \hline 10 & -4 & 16 \\ 12 & -2 & 4 \\ 13 & -1 & 1 \\ 15 & 1 & 1 \\ 20 & 6 & 36 \\ \hline sum & & 58 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    using equation (2.3.6)
    \begin{equation*} \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{58}{5}} =\sqrt{11.6} =3.4. \end{equation*}
    using equation (2.3.8)
    \begin{equation*} CSD = \frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}} =3.4/14 =0.2428. \end{equation*}
    using equation (2.3.9)
    \begin{equation*} \sigma^2 =11.6. \end{equation*}
    using equation (2.3.10)
    \begin{equation*} CV =\frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}}\times 100 = 24.28/%. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 2.3.5 Mean Deviation

    The mean deviation is the average deviation from the mean value of the given data set. The mean deviation of the data values can be easily calculated using the procedure below.
    1. Step 1: Find the mean value for the given data values
    2. Step 2: Now, subtract the mean value from each of the data values given (Note: Ignore the minus symbol)
    3. Step 3: Now, find the mean of those values obtained in step 2.
    The formula to calculate the mean deviation is given below.
    \begin{equation*} MD = \left[ \frac{\sum{\vert x-\bar{x}\vert}}{N}\right] \end{equation*}
    Here, \(X\) represents each value in the data set, \(\bar{x}\) represents the mean of the data set, \(N\) represents the number of data values, and \(|\) \(|\) represents the absolute value, which ignores the “ \(-\) ” symbol.

    Subsubsection 2.3.5.1 Mean deviation from mean

    Step 1. Find the mean: The Mean Deviation of \(3, 6, 6, 7, 8, 11, 15, 16.\)
    \begin{equation*} \bar{x}= \frac{\sum x}{N} = \frac{3+6+6+7+8+11+15+16}{8} = \frac{72}{8} =9. \end{equation*}
    Step 2. Find the distance of each value from that mean:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} x & d = |x-\bar{x}| \\ \hline 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 3 \\ 6 & 3 \\ 7 & 2 \\ 8 & 1 \\ 11 & 2 \\ 15 & 6 \\ 16 & 7 \\ \hline sum & 30 \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Step 3. Find the mean of those distances:
    \begin{equation*} MD = 30/8 = 3.75. \end{equation*}
    The values are, on average, 3.75 away from the middle.

    Subsection 2.3.6 Quartiles, Deciles, Percentiles

    Quartiles: there are three quartiles, called I quartile (or lower quartile), II quartile (or median), and III quartile (or upper quartile).
    \begin{equation} Q_1 = \text{ size of 1 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{4}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.11} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} Q_2 = \text{ size of 2 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{4}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.12} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} Q_3 = \text{ size of 3 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{4}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.13} \end{equation}
    Deciles: there are 9 deciles:
    \begin{equation} D_1 = \text{ size of 1 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{10}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.14} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} D_2 = \text{ size of 2 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{10}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.15} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} D_3 = \text{ size of 3 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{10}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.16} \end{equation}
    and so on.
    Percentiles: there are 99 deciles:
    \begin{equation} P_1 = \text{ size of 1 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{100}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.17} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} P_2 = \text{ size of 2 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{100}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.18} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} P_3 = \text{ size of 3 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{100}\right]^{th} term.\tag{2.3.19} \end{equation}
    and so on.

    Example 2.3.9.

    Calculate the \(3^{rd}\) quartile, \(6^{th}\) decile, and \(70^{th}\) percentile of the following data:
    \begin{equation*} 28,17,12,25,26,19,13,27,21,16,29 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Arrange the data in ascending order as
    \begin{equation*} 12,13,16,17,19,21,25,26,27,28,29 \end{equation*}
    Number of item, \(n = 11.\)
    Using (2.3.13)
    \begin{align*} Q_3 \amp = \text{ size of 3 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{4}\right]^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of 3 } \left[ \frac{11+1}{4}\right]^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of 3 } \left[ 3^{rd}\right] \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of } 9^{th} \text{ item} = 27. \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} D_6 \amp = \text{ size of 6 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{10}\right]^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of 6 } \left[ \frac{11+1}{10}\right]^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of 6 } \left[ 1.2^{th}\right] \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of } 7.2^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of } 7^{th} \text{ item} + 0.2\times \text{ size of } \left[8^{th} \text{ item} - 7^{th} \text{ item} \right] \\ \amp = 25 + 0.2\times \left[26 - 25\right] \\ \amp = 25 + 0.2\times 1 \\ \amp = 25.2 \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} P_{70} \amp = \text{ size of 70 } \left[ \frac{n+1}{100}\right]^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of 70 } \left[ \frac{11+1}{100}\right]^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of 70 } \left[ 0.12^{th}\right] \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of } 8.4^{th} \text{ item} \\ \amp = \text{ size of } 8^{th} \text{ item} + 0.4\times \text{ size of } \left[9^{th} \text{ item} - 8^{th} \text{ item} \right] \\ \amp = 26 + 0.4\times \left[27 - 26\right] \\ \amp = 26.4 \end{align*}

    Section 3.3 Example A

    Example 3.3.1.

    A die is rolled. Let E be the event "die shows 4" and F be the event "die shows evern number". Are E and F mutually exclusive event?
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} S \amp =\{1,2,3,4,5,6\} \\ E \amp =\{4\} \\ F \amp = \{2, 4, 6\} \\ E \cap F \amp =\{4 \} \neq \phi \end{align*}
    Therefore E and F are not mutually exclusive events.

    1. A: a number less than 7
    2. B: a number greater than 7
    3. C: a multiple of 3
    4. D: a number less than 4
    5. E: an even number greater than 4.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} S \amp =\{ 1,2,3,4,5,6\} \\ A \amp = \{1,2,3,4,5,6 \} \\ B \amp = \phi \\ C \amp = \{3,6\} \\ D \amp = \{1,2,3\} \\ E \amp = \{6\} \end{align*}

    1. A: the sum greater than 8.

    Example 3.3.8.

    Find the probability of getting 3 on rolling a die.
    Solution.
    Sample Space = \(S = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}\)
    Total number of outcomes = \(n(S) = 6\)
    Let A be the event of getting 3. Then, Number of favourable outcomes = \(n(A) = 1\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} i.e. A = \{3\} \end{equation*}
    Probability, \(P(A) = \frac{n(A)}{n(S)} = \frac{1}{6}\)
    Hence, P(getting 3 on rolling a die) = 1/6.

    Example 3.3.9.

    \(P(E) = \frac{n(A)}{n(S)} = \frac{^{12}C_1}{^{52}C_1} = 12/52 = 3/13\)
    P(the card drawn is a face card) = 3/13.

    1. \begin{equation*} P(E \text{ or } F), \end{equation*}

    Example 3.3.22.

    The probability that a student will pass the final exam in both English and Spanish is 0.5 and the probability of passing neither is 0.1. If the probability of passing the English exam is 0.75, what is the probability of passing the Spanish exam?
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} P(E \cap S) \amp = 0.5\\ P(E \cup S)' \amp = 0.1\\ P(E) \amp = 0.75 \\ P(S) \amp =? \end{align*}

    Example 3.3.23.

    In a class of 60 students, 30 opted for english (E), 32 opted for spanish (S), and 24 opted for both E and S. If one of these students is selected at random, find the probability that
    1. The student opted for the E or S.
    2. The student has opted neither E nor S.
    3. The student has opted S but not E.
    4. The student has opted only for S.
    Solution.
    1. P(E or S)
      \begin{align*} P(E \cup S) \amp = P(E) +P(S)-P(E \cap S) \\ \amp = \frac{30}{60} + \frac{32}{60} - \frac{24}{60} = \frac{38}{60}. \end{align*}
    2. neither E nor S
      \begin{align*} P(E \cup S)' \amp = 1-P(E \cup S) \\ \amp = 1- \frac{38}{60} = \frac{22}{60}. \end{align*}
    3. P (S but not E) or P (only for E)
      \begin{align*} P(S \cap E') \amp = P(S)-P(S \cap E) \\ \amp = \frac{32}{60} - \frac{24}{60} = \frac{8}{60}. \end{align*}
    4. P (only for S)
      \begin{align*} P(S \cap E') \amp = P(S)-P(S \cap E) \\ \amp = \frac{32-24}{60} = \frac{8}{60}. \end{align*}

    The probability of A winning the prize in a contest is \(1/5\text{,}\) while the probability of B winning the prize is \(3/4\text{,}\) find the probability that both winning the prizes?
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c} \circ & Win \\ \hline A & 1/5 \\ B & 3/4 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Both winning the prize = \(1/5\times 3/4 = 3/20.\)

    Example 3.3.31.

    The probability of team A participates in a game is 25%, while the probability of team B participating is 30%. What is the chances of neither of them taking part?
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|cc} \circ & participate & \text{ No participate} \\ \hline A & 25\% & 75\% \\ B & 30\% & 70\% \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Neither participate,
    \begin{equation*} P(N) = 0.75\times 0.7 = 0.525 = 52.5\% \end{equation*}

    Example 3.3.32.

    Find the probability of getting 53 Mondays in a leap year.
    Solution.
    In a leap year total number of days = 366 days = 52 weaks + 2 days. Hence, the probablity of getting 53 Mondays is
    \begin{equation*} P(M) = 2/7. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.3.33.

    Three students try to solve a problem independetly with a probability of solving it as 1/3, 2/5, and 5/12 respectively. What is the probability that the problem is solved?
    Solution.
    Probability that the problem cannot be solved is
    \begin{equation*} P(N) = \frac{2}{3}\times \frac{3}{5}\times \frac{7}{12} = \frac{7}{30} \end{equation*}
    Hence, the probability of problem solved is
    \begin{equation*} P(S) = 1-P(N) = 1- \frac{7}{30} = 23/30. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.3.34.

    Two dies are thrown simultaneously, and the sum of the numbers obtained is found to be 7. What is the probability that the number 3 has appeared at least once?
    Hint.
    Use conditional probability.
    \begin{align*} A \amp = \{(3,1),(3,2),(3,3),(3,4), (3,5),(3,6),(1,3),(2,3),(4,3),(5,3),(6,3) \} \\ B \amp = \{(1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1) \} \\ P(A) \amp = 11/36\\ P(B) \amp = 6/36\\ P(A|B) \amp = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} = 1/3. \end{align*}

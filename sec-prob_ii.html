    Section 3.2 Rules of Probability

    1. Additional Rule: It is used to calculate the probability that either (or both) of two events will happen. Notation: \(P(A \cup B), P(A + B), P(A \text{ or } B), P( \text{ either} A \text { or} B), P(\text{ at least } 1).\)
      1. For mutually exclusive (disjoint) events (A) and (B) [events that cannot happen at the same time], the probability of (A) or (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B).\tag{3.2.1} \end{equation}
        For example: tossing a coin once .
        The probability of getting a Head or a Tail on tossing a coin.
        \begin{equation*} P(H \cup T) = P(H) + P(T) = \frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2} = 1. \end{equation*}
      2. For non-mutually exclusive events (A) and (B) [events that can happen at the same time], the probability of (A) or (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)-P(A\cap B).\tag{3.2.2} \end{equation}
        For example: drawing a king of heart from a deck of card.
        The probability of getting a King of Heart on drawing a card from a deck of cards.
        \begin{equation*} P(K \cup H) = P(K) + P(H) -P(K\cap H) = \frac{4}{52}+\frac{13}{52}-\frac{1}{52} = \frac{16}{52}. \end{equation*}
    2. Multiplication Rule: It allows us to calculate the joint probability of multiple events occurring together using known probabilities of those events individually.
      1. For Independent events (A) and (B) [events where occurance of one does not affect the other], the probability of (A) and (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B).\tag{3.2.3} \end{equation}
        For example: tossing a coin twice. Tossing a coin first time does not affect what it comes in the second time. In independent events, total number of outcomes do not reduce.
        The probability of getting a 1 and then a 3 on rolling a fair dice.
        \begin{equation*} P(1 \cap 3) = P(1) \times P(3) = \frac{1}{6}\times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}. \end{equation*}
      2. For dependent events (A) and (B) [events where occurance of one does affect the other], the probability of (A) and (B) occuring is
        \begin{equation} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A).\tag{3.2.4} \end{equation}
        Where \(P(B|A)\) is a conditional probability of (B) given (A). For example: Drawing I card affect the drawing of the II card without replacement. In dependent events, total number of outcomes reduce.
        The probability of getting a King and then a Queen on drawing cards from a deck without replacement.
        \begin{equation*} P(K \cap Q) = P(K) \times P(Q/A) = \frac{4}{52}\times \frac{4}{51} = \frac{4}{2652}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.1.

    Whats the probability of drawing either a heart or a spade card from deck of cards?
    Solution.
    In this case, you cannot get a card that is both a heart and a spade. There are 13 hearts, 13 spade cards. From (3.2.1), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) = \frac{13}{52}+\frac{13}{52} = \frac{26}{52}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.2.

    What is the probability of drawing a card from a deck and getting either a diamond or a spade?
    Solution.
    In this case, you cannot get a card that is both a diamond and a spade. There are 13 diamonds and 13 spade cards. From (3.2.1), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) = \frac{13}{52}+\frac{13}{52} = \frac{26}{52}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.3.

    What is the probability of drawing a card from a deck and getting either an Ace or a King?
    Solution.
    In this case, you cannot get a card that is both an Ace and a king. There are 4 Ace and 4 king cards. From (3.2.1), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) = \frac{4}{52}+\frac{4}{52} = \frac{8}{52}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.4.

    Whats the probability of drawing either a queen or a black card from deck of cards?
    Solution.
    In this case, you can get a card that is both a queen and a black, so we need to subtract that probability to avoid double counting. There are 4 queens, 26 black cards, and two queen of black cards. From (3.2.2), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)-P(A\cap B) = \frac{4}{52}+\frac{26}{52}-\frac{2}{52} = \frac{28}{52}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.5.

    Whats the probability of drawing either a queen or a heart from deck of cards?
    Solution.
    In this case, you can get a card that is both a queen and a heart (the queen of hearts!) so we need to subtract that probability to avoid double counting. There are 4 queens, 13 hearts, and one queen of hearts. From (3.2.2), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)-P(A\cap B) = \frac{4}{52}+\frac{13}{52}-\frac{1}{52} = \frac{16}{52}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.6.

    What is the probability of flipping a fair coin and getting "heads" twice in a row?
    Solution.
    In this case, flipping a coin first time would not affect the flipping the same coin on second time, so it is an independent event. Hence, from (3.2.3), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2}=\frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.7.

    What is the probability of flipping a fair coin and getting "head" at first flip and "tail" at the second flip?
    Solution.
    In this case, flipping a coin first time would not affect the flipping the same coin on second time, so it is an independent event. Hence, from (3.2.3), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2}=\frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.8.

    From two packs of playing cards, what is the probability of drawing an "ACE" from the first pack and drawing a "king" from the second pack?
    Solution.
    From (3.2.3), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{4}{52} \times \frac{4}{52}=\frac{1}{169}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.9.

    From a single pack of playing cards, what is the probability of drawing an "ACE" at first draw and a "king" from the second draw without replacement?
    Solution.
    This a dependent event where first action does affect the second action. Hence from (3.2.4), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A) = \frac{4}{52} \times \frac{4}{51}=\frac{4}{663}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.10.

    In rolling two fair dices, what is the probability of getting 3 on both the dices?.
    Solution.
    From (3.2.3), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6}=\frac{1}{36}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.11.

    From a pack of playing cards, what is the probability that both cards selected are black without replacement?
    Solution.
    From (3.2.4), we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A) = \frac{26}{52} \times \frac{25}{51}=0.245. \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.2.1 Conditional probability:

    It is the possibility of an event that based on the existence of a previous event or outcome. In other words, The probability of occurrence of any event A when another event B in relation to A has already occurred is known as conditional probability. It is depicted by \(P(A|B)\text{,}\) here "|" read as given. So \(P(A|B)\) reads as probability of A given B.
    Imagine a student is absent from school twice a week, excluding Sunday. If it is known that he will be absent from school on Tuesday, then what are the chances that he will also be absent on Friday in the same week? It is observed that in problems where the occurrence of one event affects the happening of the following event, these cases of probability are known as conditional probability.
    \begin{align} P(A|B) \amp = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}; \quad P(B) \neq 0. \quad \text{ conditional Prob.}\tag{3.2.5}\\ or, \quad P(A|B) \amp = \frac{N(A\cap B)}{N(B)}; \quad N(B) \neq 0. \quad \text{ conditional Prob.} \tag{3.2.6} \end{align}
    here \(N(A\cap B)\) is the number of elements common to both A and B. \(N(B)\) is the number of elements in B, and it cannot be equal to zero.

    Subsection 3.2.2 Bayes Theorem:

    Bayes theorem defines the probability of occurrence of an event associated with any condition. This is known as the formula for the likelihood of causes.
    From conditional probability,
    \begin{equation*} P(A|B) = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} or, \, \quad P(A\cap B) = P(A|B).P(B)\tag{3.2.7} \end{equation}
    Also,
    \begin{equation*} P(B|A) = \frac{P(B\cap A)}{P(A)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} or, \, \quad P(B\cap A) = P(B|A).P(A)\tag{3.2.8} \end{equation}
    But,
    \begin{equation*} P(A\cap B) = P(B\cap A) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} \therefore \quad P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}\tag{3.2.9} \end{equation}
    Eqn. (3.2.9) is known as Bayes theorem.

    Example 3.2.12.

    Suppose a manufacture company has 3 suppliers A, B, and C. The supplier A give \(25\%\text{,}\) supplier B give \(40\%\text{,}\) and supplier C give \(35\%\) of some raw materials to the company to manufacture some electronic good. If \(2\%\) of the material coming from supplier A is defective, \(3\%\) of the material coming from supplier B is defective, and \(5\%\) of the material coming from supplier C is defective. If a final product is randomly chosen,
    1. what is the probability that this product be defective.
    2. what is the probability that this product be defective due to supplier B raw materials.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} P(A) \amp = 0.25 \\ P(B) \amp = 0.40 \\ P(C) \amp = 0.35 \end{align*}
    Also,
    \begin{align*} P(D|A) \amp = 0.02 \\ P(D|B) \amp = 0.03 \\ P(D|C) \amp = 0.05 \end{align*}
    1. Probability that this product is defective
      \begin{align*} P(D) \amp = P(A)\times P(D|A)+P(B)\times P(D|B)+P(C)\times P(D|C) \\ \amp = 0.25\times 0.02 +0.4\times 0.03+0.35\times 0.05 = 0.0345 \end{align*}
    2. Probability that this product is defective due to supplier B raw materials
      \begin{equation*} P(B|D) = \frac{P(D|B)P(B)}{P(D)} = \frac{0.03\times 0.40}{0.0345} = \frac{0.012}{0.0345} = 0.35. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.13.

    In 2024, there are three candidates X, Y, and Z, for the post of professor, whose chances of getting the promotion are in the proportions of \(4:2:3, \) respectively. The probability that X if selected would introduce new subject of todays requirement in the department is 0.35. The probability of Y and Z doing the same would be 0.52 and 0.80, respectively. What is the probability that there will be new subjects in the department in year 2025.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} P(X) \amp = \frac{4}{9} \\ P(Y) \amp = \frac{2}{9} \\ P(Z) \amp = \frac{3}{9} = \frac{1}{3} \end{align*}
    Also,
    \begin{align*} P(N|X) \amp = 0.35 \\ P(N|Y) \amp = 0.52 \\ P(N|Z) \amp = 0.80 \end{align*}
    Probability that New subject is introduced is
    \begin{align*} P(N) \amp = P(X)\times P(N|X)+P(Y)\times P(N|Y)+P(Z)\times P(N|Z) \\ \amp = \frac{4}{9} \times 0.35 +\frac{2}{9}\times 0.52 +\frac{1}{3}\times 0.80 = \frac{4.84}{9} \end{align*}
    Probability that New subject is introduced because of professor X
    \begin{equation*} P(X|N) = \frac{P(N|X)P(X)}{P(N)} = \frac{\frac{4}{9} \times 0.35}{\frac{4.84}{9}} = 0.29. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.14.

    There are 3 bixes, where Box A contains 1 white, 2 red, and 3 black balls, Box B contains 2 white, 3 red, and 1 black balls, and Box C contains 3 white, 1 red, and 2 black balls. A box is chosen randomly and from it two balls are taken at random. These two balls are one red and one white. Find the probability that these balls are taken from Box A.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} P(A) \amp = \frac{1}{3} \\ P(B) \amp = \frac{1}{3} \\ P(C) \amp = \frac{1}{3} \end{align*}
    Also,
    \begin{align*} P(RW|A) \amp = \frac{_1C_1\times _2C_1}{_6C_2} = \frac{2}{15}\\ P(RW|B) \amp = \frac{_3C_1\times _2C_1}{_6C_2} = \frac{6}{15}\\ P(RW|C) \amp = \frac{_3C_1\times _1C_1}{_6C_2} = \frac{3}{15} \end{align*}
    Probability of getting red and white balls
    \begin{align*} P(RW) \amp = P(A)\times P(RW|A) +P(B)\times P(RW|B) +P(C)\times P(RW|C) \\ \amp = \frac{2}{15} \times \frac{1}{3} +\frac{6}{15} \times \frac{1}{3}+\frac{3}{15} \times \frac{1}{3} = \frac{11}{45} \end{align*}
    Probability these balls are from Box A is
    \begin{equation*} P(A|RW) = \frac{P(RW|A)P(A)}{P(RW)} = \frac{\frac{2}{15} \times \frac{1}{3}}{\frac{11}{45}} = \frac{2}{11}. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.2.15.

    A newly constructed bridge fell down either due to wrong design or by inferior building material used in the construction. The chance that the design is faulty is \(10\%\) and the probability of its collapsing due faulty design is \(95\%\) and due to inferior material is \(45\%\text{.}\) Find the probability that it was due to faulty design.
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} P(FD) \amp = 0.1 \\ P(IM) \amp = 1-0.1=0.9 \end{align*}
    Also,
    \begin{align*} P(C|FD) \amp = 0.95\\ P(C|IM) \amp = 0.45 \end{align*}
    Probability collapse due to faulty design is
    \begin{equation*} P(FD|C) = \frac{ P(C|FD)P(FD)}{P(C)} = \frac{0.1\times 0.95}{0.1\times 0.95+0.9\times 0.45} = 0.19. \end{equation*}
